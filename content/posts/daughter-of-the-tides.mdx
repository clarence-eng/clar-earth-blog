I was born in a house that wept salt
from the ceiling. My mother wrung rice
with fingers like mangled mangroves,
black-veined and slick with storm.

The well was a wound we drank from.
At night, the sea came closer –
she sucked our sandals from under the bed,
mildew in a mother's tongue.

We screamed. They called it
weather. Men in suits mapped
our graves as coastal outlines.
They gave us tin roofs,
then tore out the sky.

I hung shirts on the ribs of drowned trees.
My sister stitched blood into our hems.
The water kept rising.
Still, the men never listened.

Now I speak flood.
My tongue spits brine and bone.
I carry children on my back
and wade into the next disaster –
as if it were mine to birth.
