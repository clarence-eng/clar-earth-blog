We came like wind from distant shores,
names still soft upon our tongues –
a flicker sparked in Chico air,
not yet a flame, but something young.

We took long walks to In-N-Out,
spilled fries and jokes across the floor –
the grease, the laughter, small shared joys
became the warmth we gathered for.

At FoodMaxx we filled up our carts,
with too much rice, too much meat –
cooked and laughed in borrowed kitchens,
and fed the glow with every feast.

The Wildcat roared. We ran full flame
through fields and streets and campus haze –
cage-free eggs at dawn, then chasing
buses through those wide golden days.

The days grew long, the nights stayed close –
each moment bright and softly bold.
We cupped the fire between our hands,
and knew it was a gift to hold.

We learned from rivers, rain, and roots,
from whispered truths and open skies –
through San Francisco's glowing hills,
and Baltimore, pensive and wise.

Then Washington, where stories met
laughter, reflection, soft-earned trust.
We faced the ending side by side –
a parting drifted down like dusk.

And slowly then, the fire waned –
our eyes burned red beneath the sky.
One by one, we left sparks behind –
but not the warmth. That didn't die.

Now embers hum beneath our ribs –
in breath, in pulse, in steady chest.
They glow in how we live and speak,
in what we seek, and how we rest.

They told us: go fast, go alone,
but far, together, hand in hand.
We glow not for a moment's blaze,
but for the light we'll always tend.

I carry this with quiet thanks –
for every meal and every face.
The flame you gave still lives in me,
and leaves a warmth I can't replace.

So when the dark feels long and still,
and silence dims what we became –
I miss you more than words can hold –
still alive,
and still aflame.
