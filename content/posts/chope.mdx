Three dollars, and the uncle filled the table:
char kway teow black with sweet sauce, cockles free,
kangkong, a soup, and then a plate set down
that came with nothing, just because. No one
asked for any of it. Nothing was counted.
He knew our faces. That was all to know.

A packet of tissue choped the seat. We know
the rule: paper owns the chair, the table,
as long as it takes. Nobody counted
the minutes we were gone. Every hand was free
to scold every child. Across the marble, one
auntie and then another set food down

on my mother's plate. She never turned it down.
The drinks-stall uncle poured before I could know
I wanted it: kopi-o, half sugar, one
cup sweating a ring into our table.
The fishball lady slid me extra, free.
You growing boy, she said. It was not counted.

Nobody there was rich. Nobody counted.
She did not write a single order down.
The ceiling fans turned slow. The soup was free,
the chilli free, the tea. What did we know?
The tables filled and filled, table to table,
the steam going up like something owed to no one.

I eat in rooms now where nobody shouts. One
hand takes the plate away, and it is counted:
the wine, the water, the bread we did not ask for, the table,
the tip. They pay me now to write it down.
It is a skill. Nobody I know
eats where I ate. The water is not free.

I have the money now. I could eat free
for a year on the company card. No one
would reach across to feed me. All I know
is the price of it. Now I have counted
it. Three dollars. They took the whole block down.
The market is a mall. There is no table.

The soup was free. The cockles were not counted.
None of it travels. No one wrote it down.
I did not know I was leaving that table.
